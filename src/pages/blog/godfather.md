This past summer, I got around to watching a staple of American cinema: The Godfather.  
Coming into it with healthy skepticism I was pleasantly surprised to agree that the screenplay lived up to its fame. The pacing, the characters placed perfectly in their setting, the air of nostalgia brought on by the grained-blurriness of film, all dripping in misdeeds and self-absolution of the Corleone family.

I recently finished, ["The Godfather" by Mario Puzo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Godfather_(novel)), and I found that the women feel flat in the film where they weren't in the book.

The Don's wife, Mama Corleone, goes to early mass every day. Kay had just had her firstborn and notices this when she wakes to feed the baby. Returning to greet her grandchild, she asks Kay not for the first time if she would consider converting to Catholicism. Kay in turn asks if all Catholics celebrate the Eucharist so often. Here she admits that she goes as a supplicant for her husband's soul. That she prays everyday so he goes, "up there" and not, "down there."  
This coming right after we're to understand that Michael will fully take the mantle of power in the Family.  

It wasn't often that we saw a woman in this novel be something other than

* A victim
* A bystander
* An object

In so much that her position in life allowed her to, this is Mama Corleone showing pragmaticism. While men swore fealty to the Don through fear or respect, she had sworn it through vows. The former would protect him in this realm. The latter would offer prayers in propitiation so he would be protected in the next.

This idea of absolution-by-proxy is revisited after Kay returns with Tom Hagen after having left Michael. This is after the ending we see in the film: Kay, having realized the straight-faced lie Michael told her, leaves for New Hampshire with the children (she's had her second at this point). She thought Michael especially cavalier for the murder of Carlo Rizzi after standing Godfather to his son. Tom explains how he didn't want to initially, it had been Kay's request that made him Godfather to the child - even though it was the strategic thing to do. Hearing this, she bows her head. She understands in some way she also had a hand in this.  
We end the novel with Kay in the parish cleaning herself of sin, so she can return to the altar rail and pray for Michael Corleone to go up there, and not down there.

How I wish this ending were adapted to the screen. It would've added some depth to otherwise flattened side characters. It struck me as a beautiful piece of parallelism that would've fit right in with the cognitive dissonance of murderous Catholics. It would have highlighted the small yet tangible domains that these women held, and the ways they coped with impossible truths.
