# My thoughts on The Godfather

## Jan 25, 2026

This past summer, I got around to watching a staple of American cinema: The Godfather.  
Coming into it with healthy skepticism I was pleasantly surprised to agree that the screenplay lived up to its fame. The pacing, the characters placed perfectly in their setting, the air of nostalgia brought on by the grained-blurriness of film, all dripping in misdeeds and self-absolution of the Corleone family.

I recently finished, ["The Godfather" by Mario Puzo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Godfather_(novel)), and I found that the women feel flat in the film where they weren't in the book.

The Don's wife, Mama Corleone, goes to early mass every day. Kay had just had her firstborn and notices this when she wakes to feed the baby. Returning to greet her grandchild, she asks Kay not for the first time if she would consider converting to Catholicism. Kay in turn asks if all Catholics celebrate the Eucharist so often. Here she admits that she goes as a supplicant for her husband's soul. That she prays everyday so he goes, "up there" and not, "down there."  
This coming right after we're to understand that Michael will fully take the mantle of power in the Family.  

It wasn't often that we saw a woman in this novel be something other than

* A victim
* A bystander
* An object

In so much that her position in life allowed her to, this is Mama Corleone showing pragmaticism. While men swore fealty to the Don through fear or respect, she had sworn it through vows. The former would protect him in this realm. The later would offer prayers in propitiation so he would be protected in the next.

This idea of absolution-by-proxy is revisited after Kay returns with Tom Hagan after having left Michael.
